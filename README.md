## Overview
Runaway Wheel is an infinite-runner game to challenge the player of their familiarity with a keyboard by typing out words/phrases in order to jump over obstacles that come their way. Anyone, aged 10 & up, are tasked to overcome obstacles by matching the words/phrases presented to them on screen. Games within the genre often test the user’s reflex skill, with the control scheme usually consisting of pressing one button, spacebar or tapping the screen. Runaway Wheel aims to go about the general gameplay loop of an infinite-runner in a different way, and the goal is to shake up the genre in a meanigful way.

## Learning Objective
Runaway Wheel is a personal game project of mine, it was an idea I've had rolling around in my head, no pun intended, for about over a year by now. I wanted one of my final college projects to end with a bang, one where I put the programming and art skills that I've learned throughout my college career to the test.
### My goals were..
- Understand what makes a game fun and challenging
- Undersand the underlying systems required to make a game
- Develop a stronger understanding of the C language
- Develop quick, effective development strategies
    - Project was developed using Agile Methodology
    - A sprinkle of Waterfall Methodology was in place due to the short development time
- Design assets that were appropriate for the game and genre
- Design assets that were readable to the user

## Takeaways
The list of takeaways will be ever expansive, so here are a few that felt most important to me; some good, some bad.
### Some key takeaways..
- Plan, plan, PLAN ideas
    - Sketch and write every idea out
    - List out high conceptual ideas, then break them down into smaller components
        - This would be especially useful for game mechanics/functions
- Think of the beginning and end state of the project early
    - This was a huge mistake on my part
    - What I ended up with was fine, but could use improvement
- Games are hard, but fun!
    - Unity, Unreal, Godot, etc. I realized how much I took simple things for granted
    - Simple features were major development sinks, had to pick and choose appropiately
- Having assets mostly done made development easier, but that won't always be the case
- Finish that 10%
    - For this game, I would say 5% instead
    - Should've added those features that would really make the game feel alive
        - Sound/Music
        - Animations
        - Scoreboard
        - No repeat prompts
- Playtest MORE!!!
    - I was thankful to have two opportunities to playtest with a bunch of people
    - Provided me the most insight on where I should direct the project
    - Helped me see flaws that I wouldn't see if I did a playthrough
- Small is good, but not forever
    - I have ambitions (very large ones infact)
    - Small games are great for the low-level tech stack I give myself
    - Allows me to experiement quickly
    - Allows me to hit the wall and make mistakes without much consequence/time sink
    - Prepares me for bigger and better endeavors
- Low-level is good (for me)
    - Helps me cover programming topics and actually apply them in a meaningful way
    - Teaches me to be patient
    - Allows me to think outside the box
    - Solutions aren't just handed to me by a higher level function, I have to think!
    - I don't need to license an engine to make my games, and I can be independent from coporate gaming overlords
